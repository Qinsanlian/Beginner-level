# Two-Year Side Quest v1: A Growth System That Does Not Overlap with the Main Skill Tree

## 0. Positioning

This document is not a technical roadmap and does not replace `stage1_skill_tree.txt`.

The main skill tree covers: programming, engineering, English evidence, projects, troubleshooting, cloud, observability, job-seeking evidence.

This document only covers side‑quest capabilities:
- Physical endurance
- Mental resilience
- Time & execution system
- Information source management
- Career reconnaissance
- Interpersonal & communication skills
- Public documentation & project storytelling
- Financial & life risk control
- Interview rhythm & application discipline
- Fallback management

Principle: side quests serve only the main quest, not compete for its time.

---

## 1. Side Quest Overall Goal

Within two years, build a stable personal operating system that allows you to sustainably complete the main skill tree – not by short bursts of passion.

Final state:
- Body can withstand long‑term learning and internship rhythm
- Emotions do not overturn your whole life plan because of a single bad day
- Information intake is not contaminated by anxiety‑inducing content
- You consistently record progress, but never become a mentor‑style preacher
- You can identify quality of roles, teams, companies, and opportunities
- You manage fallbacks with realistic constraints, not letting them spoil you
- In applications, interviews, and communication, you appear reliable, clear‑headed, and collaborative

---

## 2. Side Quest A: Physical Endurance System

### Goal

Not to become an athlete, but to ensure two years of intense growth are not derailed by physical breakdown.

### Daily minimum

- Keep sleep as stable as possible; do not use late nights to prove effort
- At least 20‑30 minutes of walking or low‑intensity activity every day
- At least 3 strength or core sessions per week, 20‑40 minutes each
- During long study sessions, stand up and move every 60‑90 minutes
- Control sugary drinks, binge eating, and prolonged sitting

### Monthly gate

Record each month:
- Average sleep duration
- Number of exercise sessions per week
- Whether there have been 3+ consecutive days of severe fatigue
- Whether physical condition caused interruption of main quest learning

### Red lines

If any of the following persist for two consecutive weeks, actively downshift:
- Significantly broken sleep
- Persistent palpitations, headaches, stomach pain, or physical discomfort
- Inability to focus during the day
- Clearly out‑of‑control mood swings

Downshifting is not failure – it is a strategy to protect two‑year continuity.

---

## 3. Side Quest B: Mental Resilience & Anti‑Anxiety System

### Goal

Break down “hydraulic‑press anxiety” into executable actions, so anxiety does not devour execution.

### Basic rules

1. Do not make major life decisions late at night.
2. Do not rewrite the roadmap because of one low‑output day.
3. Do not use “I’m finished” as a retrospective conclusion.
4. Do not shame yourself with others’ progress.
5. Do not crush today’s basic practice with the ultimate goal.

### Daily 5‑line retrospective

Write only 5 lines each day:
- What did I accomplish today?
- Where did I get stuck today?
- What is the smallest action for tomorrow?
- Was I carried away by anxiety today?
- Do I need to pause or ask for help?

### Stress levels

Level 1: ordinary anxiety
- Response: keep doing the smallest task of the day.

Level 2: obvious mental drain
- Response: halve the tasks, keep only the minimum closed loop.

Level 3: insomnia, panic, persistent feelings of breakdown
- Response: pause roadmap analysis, talk to a real person, seek professional help if necessary.

Level 4: thoughts of self‑harm or not wanting to live
- Response: immediately contact a trusted person, family member, teacher, or local emergency services – do not stay alone.

---

## 4. Side Quest C: Time & Execution System

### Goal

Make “8 hours a day” not a slogan, but sustainable effective time.

### Time block design

Suggest splitting each day into 4 types of time:
- Deep block: main quest learning or project work
- Maintenance block: organising, reviewing, archiving, planning
- Input block: reading, courses, documentation, job market information
- Recovery block: exercise, eating, sleep, screen‑free rest

### Daily minimum closed loop

Good days:
- 2 deep blocks
- 1 input block
- 1 maintenance block

Low‑energy days:
- 1 minimal deep block
- 1 retrospective entry
- Do not add new plans

### Weekly retrospective template

Answer only these at the end of each week:
- Did the main quest advance this week?
- Which side quest affected the main quest?
- Was there any useless input?
- What should be removed next week?
- What is the single most important thing for next week?

---

## 5. Side Quest D: Information Sources & Cognitive Hygiene

### Goal

Reduce anxiety‑driven information intake and build high‑quality professional/technical sources.

### Keep

Only keep information that leads to action:
- Official documentation
- High‑quality engineering blogs
- Real incident post‑mortems
- Job descriptions (JDs)
- Open‑source issue / PR discussions
- English technical articles
- Experience records from credible practitioners

### Limit

Consume the following only in limited amounts each week:
- Overnight‑success stories
- Salary bragging
- Big‑tech myths
- AI anxiety content
- “Master in months” tutorials
- Short videos selling roadmaps

### Monthly information source cleanup

Each month, delete or mute:
- Accounts that make you anxious without leading to action
- Content that makes you fantasise about shortcuts
- Content that makes you obsess over comparison
- Content that only talks about grand trends without evidence or execution

## 6. Side Quest E: Career Reconnaissance System

### Goal

Gradually understand real job requirements over two years, instead of choosing directions by imagination.

### Roles to scout

Observe only, do not rush to apply:
- Backend development internship
- Ops development internship
- DevOps internship
- Test development internship
- Technical support with automation focus
- Cloud platform support roles
- Junior technical roles at foreign companies
- Infrastructure‑related internships at mid‑size companies

### Monthly JD study

Each month, collect 10 real JDs and record:
- Job title
- Company type
- Must‑have requirements
- Nice‑to‑have items
- Degree restrictions
- English requirements
- Whether the role is more development, operations, support, testing, or platform
- Your current gap

### Quarterly job map

Every 3 months, update:
- Which roles are becoming attainable?
- Which roles are still out of reach?
- Which requirements appear frequently together?
- Which terms are just noise?
- How should the main quest projects align next quarter?

### Quarterly external calibration

Each quarter, share the job map with a trusted external perspective – can be me or someone in the industry who understands.

The goal is not to be judged, but to help you distinguish:
- Real gaps
- Things you are worrying about prematurely
- Requirements already covered by your current main quest
- JDs that are just word‑stacking or screening noise
- Capabilities you need to add evidence for next quarter

You have been under “hydraulic‑press pressure” for a long time, so you must regularly introduce external perspective. Its value is to remind you: some JD requirements are already being satisfied – you do not need to negate yourself just because you see them.

### Red line

Do not browse JDs every day to scare yourself. JD research is reconnaissance, not self‑judgment.

## 7. Side Quest F: Interpersonal Skills, Communication & Foreign‑Company Fit

### Goal

Cultivate a reliable, clear, collaborative communication style, preparing for internships and foreign‑company environments.

### Basic communication principles

- State facts, do not exaggerate
- State limitations, do not pretend to know
- State progress, do not speak in vague terms
- Provide context when describing a problem
- Before asking for help, organise what you have already tried
- Accept feedback, do not rush to defend
- Do not dump high‑pressure emotions on collaborators

### Monthly communication training

Each month, complete:
- 3‑minute project explanation in Chinese
- 3‑minute English self‑introduction draft
- 1 mock help request: background, problem, attempts, evidence, request
- 1 mock project status update: accomplishments, risks, next steps

### Key points for foreign‑company fit

Gradually develop over two years:
- Clearly articulate problems
- Respect documentation and processes
- Work asynchronously
- Write clear context
- Be honest about uncertainty
- Be careful with commitments

## 8. Side Quest G: Public Documentation & Project Storytelling

### Goal

Document growth, but never become a mentor‑style preacher.

### Positioning

You are not a mentor now. You do not output “how to succeed” lessons.

You can output:
- Apprentice notes
- Pitfall records
- Retrospectives
- Project progress
- Mistake corrections
- Real limitations

### Weekly public post

At most one post per week, to avoid content creation cannibalising the main quest.

Format:
- What did I do this week?
- What problem did I encounter?
- What is the evidence?
- Where was I wrong before?
- What will I do next week?

### Forbidden content

- Grand life advice
- Technical roadmap preaching
- Writing experience summaries before delivering something real
- Passing AI‑generated content as personal insight
- Exchanging anxious stories for attention

### Two‑year goal

Leave a credible growth trail:
- Not packaging yourself as an expert
- But showing that you have become increasingly reliable

---

## 9. Side Quest H: Financial & Life Risk Control

### Goal

Prevent life risks from interrupting the main quest, and avoid relaxing discipline just because fallbacks exist.

### Basic principles

- Having a fallback does not mean taking it
- A fallback is a cushion, not an identity
- Do not actively burn resources to prove independence
- Do not let resources live your life for you
- Use resources to buy training time, not laziness

### Quarterly check

Every 3 months, review:
- Current living costs
- Whether learning equipment is sufficient
- Whether there is unnecessary spending
- Whether a part‑time job or internship is needed
- Whether a part‑time job or internship would break the main quest rhythm
- Whether you have at least 1‑3 months of buffer

### Red lines

- Do not use spending to relieve anxiety
- Do not buy equipment for face
- Do not reject reasonable support just to prove yourself
- Do not take low‑quality work that clearly consumes the main quest, unless survival depends on it

## 10. Side Quest I: Interview & Application Rhythm

### Goal

Prepare for job seeking early, but do not enter mass‑application anxiety prematurely.

### Months 0‑6

Do not focus on applications, only observation:
- Collect JDs
- Record requirements
- Understand internship processes
- Draft a personal introduction

### Months 7‑12

Start low‑intensity trials:
- Find 3‑5 low‑risk roles to practice applications
- Record resume feedback
- Record interview questions
- Do not reject the roadmap because of failures

### Months 13‑18

Start formal preparation:
- 1 mock interview per month
- Update resume monthly
- Prepare project walkthrough script
- Prepare English self‑introduction

### Months 19‑24/27

Enter main application period:
- Fixed weekly application window
- Retrospective after each interview
- Distinguish rejection reasons: degree, projects, expression, mismatch, timing
- Do not spam applications, do not let rejection letters emotionally overwhelm you

---

## 11. Side Quest J: Fallback Management & Identity Boundaries

### Goal

Neither demonise fallbacks nor let them spoil you.

### Definition of fallback

A fallback is not the path you will take – it is insurance for your training window.

Correct use:
- Lower survival anxiety
- Buy learning time
- Support low‑pay but high‑growth internships
- Allow long‑term choices

Incorrect use:
- Fantasise about falling back when facing difficulties
- Use the fallback to comfort yourself into procrastination
- Manufacture self‑adulation by “not taking the fallback”
- Refuse all resources just to prove independence

### Semi‑annual self‑check

Answer:
- Have I lowered execution because a fallback exists?
- Have I tortured myself excessively because I do not want to take the fallback?
- Have I used resources for training rather than escape?
- If the fallback did not exist, would my current abilities allow more self‑reliance than six months ago?

---

## 12. Side Quest K: Real Feedback System

### Goal

Continuously expose yourself to real feedback, avoiding closed‑door planning.

### Feedback sources

Build over two years:
- Project feedback from classmates or friends
- Feedback from online technical communities
- Resume revision feedback
- Mock interview feedback
- Internship application feedback
- Open‑source community interaction feedback
- English expression feedback

### Feedback record format

For each feedback, record only:
- Source
- What problem was pointed out
- Whether this problem has appeared repeatedly
- Whether it needs to become a next‑week action
- If not adopted, why

### Note

Feedback is not a verdict. Feedback is just calibration.

## 13. Two‑Year Side Quest Timeline

### Months 0‑3

Focus: stable start
- Build sleep, exercise, retrospective habits
- Clean up anxiety‑inducing information sources
- Collect 10 JDs per month
- Create a weekly public post template
- Do not rush interviews, do not rush to prove yourself

### Months 4‑6

Focus: rhythm stabilisation
- Weekly retrospective becomes regular
- First job map compilation
- Start 3‑minute project explanation training
- Build basic financial/life buffer awareness

### Months 7‑12

Focus: get feedback
- Low‑risk application trials
- Ask someone to review a resume draft
- Do 1‑2 mock interviews
- Shift public posts from “what I learned” to “what problem I solved”

### Months 13‑18

Focus: professional expression
- Monthly mock interview
- Monthly resume update
- Project walkthrough script starts taking shape
- Regular training on English self‑introduction and project explanation
- Decide which entry path suits you best: backend, ops development, test development, DevOps, or automation‑focused technical support

### Months 19‑24/27

Focus: application & calibration
- Fixed application rhythm
- Retrospective after each interview
- Fine‑tune resume and walkthrough based on feedback
- Do not overturn the main quest for short‑term results
- Among opportunities that provide growth, prioritise those with high problem quality and feedback density

## 14. Side Quest Acceptance Criteria

After two years, you should have:

- Stable sleep schedule and basic exercise habits
- Ability to avoid frequently overturning plans due to anxiety
- Continuous, credible weekly retrospective records
- At least 12 months of job reconnaissance records
- At least 4 quarterly job maps
- At least 6 mock or real interview retrospectives
- At least 12 apprentice‑style public posts or private growth logs
- A clear personal introduction
- A professional orientation you can explain
- A sustainable application and interview retrospective system
- Clear boundaries with fallbacks: use them to buy training windows, but not to define yourself

## 16. Final Principle

The main quest determines whether you get hired.

The side quests determine whether you can sustain yourself until that day.

Do not use side quests to escape the main quest, and do not neglect side quests to the point of main‑quest collapse.

A true two‑year plan is not about burning every day – it is about becoming stronger in a stable, clear‑headed, verifiable way.
